Note: detailed fusion battle math is still handled in the battling app. This doc is the **campaign-facing rules layer**: what fusion is, how the device works, how to pick a fused form at the table, and how defusing works in play.

## What fusion is (campaign fiction)
In warped zones, biological and spatial rules misbehave. Under certain conditions:
- Two Pokémon can merge into **one shared body**.
- Both original minds can remain present (often cooperative).
- The fused form can express blended traits, typings, and move access.

Fusion is not treated as an everyday safe trainer tool. It is an unstable seam-era phenomenon that the party can sometimes stabilize, survive, study, or undo.

## What players should expect from fusion scenes

Fusion scenes should feel like one of these:
- a boss or anomaly the party has to survive
- a consent-heavy experimental sync scene with real risk
- a rescue / save-it scene where defusing matters more than winning
- a weird-but-beautiful route event that shows how broken the world is

## Picking the fused form at the table

When the party needs a concrete fused Pokémon form, use a fusion visual/reference tool first, then resolve stats in the app.

Recommended workflow:
1. pick the two source Pokémon
2. use **Pokémon Infinite Fusion's Pokémon Fusion Tool** to generate a fast fused species look/name reference
3. choose the version that best matches the scene tone
4. use the battling app for the actual mechanical resolution

Use the fusion tool for:
- quick art/reference
- naming the fused form
- deciding which body traits dominate the scene
- helping players understand what they are trying to save, fight, or calm

Do not let the visual tool overrule campaign tone.
- If the generated fusion is too goofy for the scene, reroll or choose another result.
- If the scene is horror, tragedy, or mythic, pick the result that supports that tone.
- If the scene is a controlled lab test, pick the cleaner or more stable-looking result.

## What the device does (field prototype)
**Fusion/Defusion Device** functions:
- **Stabilize:** reduces the chance of involuntary fusion within a small radius.
- **Defuse:** separates a fused entity back into two Pokémon.
- **Record:** captures anomaly readings (useful for research checklist rewards).

## Fusion scene setup

When you run a fusion scene, decide four things first:

1. **Cause**
  - seam pressure
  - deliberate machine use
  - weather anomaly
  - boss event
  - route fracture overlap

2. **Stability**
  - **Stable**: coherent body, shared instincts, lower collateral
  - **Shaky**: visible stress, erratic movement, emotional bleed
  - **Critical**: failing seams, harmful overlap, likely meltdown if ignored

3. **Consent state**
  - willing sync
  - confused but not hostile
  - involuntary / trapped
  - violent / panicked

4. **Goal of the scene**
  - fight it
  - calm it
  - save it
  - record it
  - escort it somewhere safe

## Trainer fusion / bond-sync lane

Battle Bond-style syncs and machine-assisted bond scenes can approach fusion territory, but they are not the same thing.

- **Battle Bond / sync** is a short-term trainer-Pokémon performance link.
- **Fusion** is two Pokémon, or a severe anomaly body-state, becoming one unstable being.

If a trainer is ever directly involved in a true fusion event, treat it as a story-tier exception with much higher risk, stricter consent, and clear aftermath.

## Defuse procedure (table-ready; locked draft)
Defusing is the “save it” option after beating a fused/rampaging boss.

Requirements:
- The target must be **incapacitated** (fainted / restrained / otherwise unable to fight back).
- You must have the **Defusing Tool**.

How it works:
- Attempts: you get **2 attempts** before the fusion destabilizes (DM can allow a 3rd with a risk).
- Primary check: **INT** (operate the tool + follow the protocol).

DC guidance (pick one method):
- Method A (tier only):
  - Variant A: DC 12
  - Variant B: DC 14
  - Variant C: DC 16
  - High Chaos day/plot: DC 18
- Method B (tier + level pressure):
  - Start with the tier DC above, then add +1 if the boss is level 51–80, +2 if 81–110, +3 if 111+.

Fusion complications (adds a secondary requirement on each attempt):
- Tree/roots: needs an **ATH or STR** assist to hold it still.
- Wind/storm: needs an **ATH** assist to pin it / prevent “slip.”
- Stone/metal: needs a **STR** assist to pry plates/anchors.
- Water/mist: needs an **FTD** assist to endure cold/drenching backlash.
- Shadow/void: needs an **INT** assist to track the “real core” (or raise DC).

On a successful Defuse, the party earns:
- research credit
- a stabilized Pokémon (or returns it to the wild)
- intel about what caused the fusion

On a failed Defuse sequence, choose one:
- the fusion flees deeper into the route
- the body destabilizes into a harsher second phase
- the Pokémon split badly and need urgent medical help
- the fusion collapses the local route lane and creates a new hazard scene

## Stabilize procedure (mid-scene support)

If the party is not ready to fully defuse, they can try to stabilize first.

Use when:
- the fight is still ongoing
- the target is panicking but not fully hostile
- the party needs more time before the Defuse attempts

Basic stabilize action:
- 1 trainer uses the device
- make an **INT** check
- baseline **DC 12** on a Shaky fusion, **DC 15** on a Critical one

On a success, choose one:
- reduce the next Defuse DC by `2`
- suppress one dangerous fusion symptom for the scene
- prevent the fusion from fleeing or self-harming this round
- buy one extra Defuse attempt later

On a failure:
- no improvement
- one visible symptom worsens
- the next hostile action gets stronger or stranger

## Fusion symptoms

Roll `d6` or choose what the party sees.

| d6 | Symptom |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Type bleed**: the air, ground, or nearby water starts behaving like one of the fusion's types |
| 2 | **Emotional echo**: fear, rage, grief, or protectiveness spills into the area |
| 3 | **Body mismatch**: limbs, armor, fins, roots, or jaws do not agree on movement |
| 4 | **Move leakage**: scene hazards imitate one of the source Pokémon's moves |
| 5 | **Spatial drift**: the fusion occupies slightly wrong space and leaves seam afterimages |
| 6 | **Instinct conflict**: the two source drives want different things right now |

## Safety + ethics constraints (tone-setting)
- Vulmon’s lab data suggests fusion is **not inherently harmful**, but:
  - outcomes vary by species and stress level
  - prolonged instability is risky
- PCs are expected to prioritize Pokémon consent and safety.

Hard tone rules:
- willing, happy fusion should look different from trapped or violent fusion
- rescue scenes should feel rescuable
- consent matters more than novelty
- if a scene starts reading as body-horror in a way your table does not want, scale it back immediately

## Fusion mechanics
Fusion mechanics (stats/moves/command structure) are resolved by the app.

Paper-use fallback if the app is not available:
- use the fusion tool for the body/look
- use the higher HP source as the HP base
- use the stronger offensive type/theme as the primary attack lane
- give it one signature move from each source
- give it one instability trait from the symptom table above
- keep the scene moving instead of over-building the stat block

## Warped zone effects (quick table)
When entering a warped zone, roll 1d6:
1. Type resonance (one type is boosted/overrepresented)
2. Gravity mismatch (movement issues)
3. Time-lag (initiative quirks)
4. Mood bleed (Pokémon emotions amplify)
5. Fusion pressure (fusion chance increases)
6. Clean pocket (safe, but still weird)

## When to use fusion at all

Use fusion sparingly when you want one of these campaign jobs done:
- show that the world is still broken
- make a route or lab feel memorable
- present a boss that can be saved instead of just beaten
- give research factions something morally charged to chase
- tie strange weather, seam pressure, and living Pokémon behavior together
