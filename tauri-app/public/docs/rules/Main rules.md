# Stats

You have 25 points to allocate to each stat, each stat has a base of 5.

  

How ability checks work is D12 + stat/2 (Round up). Stats grant a bonus to rolls, but DCs are quite high. (15-25) On Maximum roll (Natural 12) double the stat value. So, with ten strength, a natural 12 will be a 22. (12 +2(10/2)). Divide the stat by two and round up.

  

Each player starts with a trait (see Trainer Traits).

## Pokemon field stats

If a Pokemon needs to make a direct out-of-battle action check, environmental stunt check, or showcase-style performance check, use [Pokemon Field Stats & Skill Checks.md](Pokemon%20Field%20Stats%20%26%20Skill%20Checks.md).

That system keeps normal battle math unchanged and gives Pokemon their own quick derived stat line for scene actions.

## Strength

- General physical strength actions: Lifting things, throwing things, attacking, etc. 
    

  

Strength is used in a variety of scenarios, but mostly doing that might help with traversal or when you need to force your way through something. If a wild pokemon is giving you trouble, nothing is stopping you from just hitting it with a stick and calling it a day.

## Athletics

- General athletic actions: Running, jumping, movement based actions.
    
- Effects dodge chance. (D12 + stat/2 (Round up))
    
- Effects Base Stamina (SP) (stat + 5)
    

  

Stamina determines how far you can travel in a single day without resting, and determines how long you can train without a trip back to a Pokémon Center. So, if you are attempting to travel between towns that have a mountain separating them, then it will take more stamina than going between towns with a single route between them. If you are having an intensive training session, you can go even harder with higher SP. So having low stamina will result in needing to rest more often and use more supplies.

  

Stamina can only be restored via short or long rest.

## Intelligence

- General decision making, investigation, and “reading the room” actions.
    
- Effects knowledge checks (Checks to get information that the player either cannot remember or that a well-read character might know) (D12 + stat/2)
    
- Perception checks (Seeing something that might otherwise be hidden)
- Insight checks (seeing if someone is lying, hiding something, or reading their general mood)
    

  

Intelligence is based on the mind and memory. Intelligence can be used to get pokemon type information, weaknesses and potential moves that they may know in battle. It can be used to see if a character they are talking to is lying, or to see if information they give may or may not be correct. It can also be used to check the history of an event or a place that might be important to the story or what you’re doing.

## Speech

- Social checks: persuasion, deception, intimidation, negotiation.
- Performance and presentation.

## Fortitude

- Endurance and willpower under stress.
- Resisting exhaustion, pain, fear, and harsh environments.
- Used for pushing training/travel beyond normal limits.

## Luck

Luck is used for finding specific pokemon you want and catching them with ease. This stat allows for some fun antics for you to get up to and come out unscathed, and allow for “Test Your Luck” rolls.

# Battle Bond (Trait draft)

Battle Bond is treated as a **Trait** that you can only get through the **Fusion Machine / Bond Sync** setup.

Baseline idea:
- It is **temporary** (short buff window), not a permanent mega-stat rewrite.
- It should feel strong, but come with a clear **cost** afterward.

Effect (draft):
- +1 stage to one offensive stat **and** +1 stage Speed.

Requirements (draft):
- Trainer must meet a Fortitude requirement: **15** (your body surviving the sync).
- Partner Pokémon must have **maxed happiness**.

Activation (draft):
- **Once per long rest**, you can run a Bond Sync on one Pokémon to trigger Battle Bond.

Cost (draft):
- While active, Battle Bond drains Stamina (SP) per turn based on the Pokémon’s level:
	- **Level 50 and below:** drain **1 SP/turn**
	- **Level 51+:** drain **2 SP/turn**

DM reference: `Rules/DM Rules - Level Caps, Raids, Fusion, Defuse.md`

# Insight Checks

For Insight checks, you roll **Intelligence**.

Insight is your tool for:
- reading **mood** (fear/anger/excitement)
- spotting **lies / withholding**
- reading **intent** (stalling, threatening, bargaining)

Insight is not mind-reading:
- it won’t reveal exact thoughts
- it won’t auto-solve mysteries without evidence

On a success, the DM gives you 1–2 clear tells and what they suggest.

# Type Enthusiast

At the time of character creation, you can choose to specialize in a specific type. It leads to the following benefits:

- You find them more often
    
- Have a 1.5X multiplier when catching them and with Wild Bond
    
- Have an understanding of pokemon of that type better giving you advantage on intelligence rolls when that type is involved. 
    

But doing so leads to the following nerfs

- You find other types of pokemon less, leading to less type variety
    
- Have a 0.75X Catching Multiplier when attempting to catch or Wild Bond with pokemon outside that type.
    

If you choose to not specialize in a specific type, then you get the, “Wild Card” tag to mark on your sheet. You don’t get the buffs, but you also don’t get nerfs

# Catching

Wild capture now has three main lanes:

- **Raw Catching**: throw quickly and rely on ball quality, status, and low HP.
- **Battle Catching**: weaken the target first, then throw.
- **Wild Bond**: calm, impress, feed, read, or challenge the Pokemon before the throw.

## Wild Bond

Before a battle, or during a calm encounter scene, the trainer may attempt **one Wild Bond check**.

Choose the stat that matches the approach:

- **Strength**: displays of power, protection, hauling, breaking, or direct physical proof.
- **Athletics**: racing, climbing, active play, chase, swimming, or matching movement.
- **Intelligence**: reading behavior, offering the right food, solving an environmental problem, or understanding habitat cues.
- **Speech**: calming, coaxing, bargaining, mimic calls, or direct social connection.
- **Fortitude**: enduring pain, heat, cold, fear, or discomfort to prove resolve.
- **Luck**: weird coincidence, perfect timing, strange offerings, or risky instinct.

The DM can grant advantage if the approach strongly fits the species, type, mood, or current route scene.

## Level-scaled Wild Bond DCs(baseline for common pokemon)

| Pokemon level | Straight-catch DC | Respect DC | Max Bond Bonus |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 1-10 | 12 | 8 | +2 |
| 11-20 | 14 | 10 | +3 |
| 21-30 | 16 | 12 | +3 |
| 31-40 | 18 | 14 | +4 |
| 41-50 | 20 | 16 | +4 |
| 51-60 | 22 | 18 | +5 |
| 61-70 | 24 | 20 | +5 |
| 71-80 | 26 | 22 | +6 |
| 81-90 | 28 | 24 | +6 |
| 91+ | 30 | 26 | +7 |

## Wild Bond results

- **Beat the Straight-catch DC**: if the Pokemon is not plot-locked, owned, raiding, or in a full hostile frenzy, it can be caught immediately or choose to join willingly without a full battle.
- **Meet the Respect DC but miss the Straight-catch DC**: the Pokemon is impressed but not fully won over. If a battle follows, your first catch attempt gains a **Bond Bonus** equal to `1 + half the margin above the Respect DC`, rounded down, to the band's maximum.
- **Fail the Respect DC by 1-4**: no bonus. The Pokemon decides whether to battle, flee, posture, or test the trainer another way.
- **Fail the Respect DC by 5+**: the wild scene turns against the trainer. Expect a harder battle, a retreat, or a scene complication.

## Battle and Raw Catch notes

- Wild Bond bonuses stack with ball modifiers, status bonuses, and Type Enthusiast modifiers.
- **Raw Catching** is still allowed, but it does not benefit from Wild Bond unless the party set one up first.
- If the table is using a pure catch-rate multiplier system instead of a flat catch roll, each point of **Bond Bonus** can be read as `+0.25x catch rate` instead.
- Legendary, boss, raid, fusion, or story-critical Pokemon may require battle or story resolution even on a successful Wild Bond.

- `1.5x` -> `+2`
- `2x` -> `+4`
- `3x` -> `+6`
- `3.5x` -> `+7`
- `4x` -> `+8`
- `5x` -> `+10`

Max level is 10, but any DM can go over if they wish to do so. Why? Because it’s your game.

## Pokemon Leveling

Pokemon levels can go **over level 100** in this campaign.

Pokemon level from battling and by doing miscellaneous actions (Breaking rocks, getting berries from trees, etc). It’s mostly on what moves the pokemon can know, and if the DM thinks that a certain move is far too high of a level for a pokemon to know, then they have the ability to VETO it.

TMs and HMs (Technical Machines and Hidden Machines) are also in this category, as they need to be bought, refined, or found in the wild to be used. In this campaign, most TMs are made from **source energy** harvested from type-aligned biomes and then etched into machine plates, so where a party travels matters to what they can learn. Crafted TMs are normally **one-use** unless they come from a gym, League certification, or another official multi-use source. See [TM Crafting & Etching.md](TM%20Crafting%20%26%20Etching.md), [Crafting, Salvage & Field Fabrication.md](Crafting%2C%20Salvage%20%26%20Field%20Fabrication.md), `shop prices/TMs - sword-shield (by type, PokeAPI).md`, and `players/Gimmick Research Objectives.md` for the linked progression. 

  

When directly training:

Roll a D12 and subtract that number from your SP. If you still have SP remaining you can keep rolling until you get to 0. However, points you go over your SP Max, you take that much in damage to HP. You can only regain HP with medicine, field treatment, natural recovery, or a Pokemon Center. If you go over your amount of HP and pass out, the pokemon gets zero levels.

 For example: If you have 13 SP, and roll a 4 on the D12. the SP becomes 9 and the Pokémon trained gains 1 level.

# Battling

Use the battling app you made.

If you want a tabletop fallback (or want it like the anime), use this:

### Pokemon HP

Take the Base HP stat of every pokemon, Divide it by 2 (Rounded down) and add 1 HP per pokemon level. This statistic does not apply to Shedinja. ((Base HP Stat / 2) + 1(Level))

### Pokemon Attacking and Defending
Use the Pokemon's **calculated battle stats at its current level**, not its base stats.

If you are using the app, use the calculated Attack, Defense, Special Attack, Special Defense, and Speed values it gives you.

#### Move power to damage die

- Adjusted power `0-29`: `1d4`
- Adjusted power `30-49`: `1d6`
- Adjusted power `50-69`: `1d8`
- Adjusted power `70-89`: `1d10`
- Adjusted power `90-109`: `1d12`
- Adjusted power `110+`: `1d20`

#### Calculated combat bonus bands

Use these bands for the attacking stat, defensive stat, and speed contests:

| Calculated stat | Attack / Sp. Atk bonus | Defense / Sp. Def modifier | Speed bonus |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| `0-39` | `+0` | `+1 damage taken` | `+0` |
| `40-59` | `+1` | `0` | `+1` |
| `60-79` | `+2` | `-1` | `+2` |
| `80-99` | `+3` | `-2` | `+3` |
| `100-119` | `+4` | `-3` | `+4` |
| `120-139` | `+5` | `-4` | `+5` |
| `140-159` | `+6` | `-5` | `+6` |
| `160+` | `+7` | `-6` | `+7` |

#### Head-on move clash power bonus

When two moves collide directly instead of one just hitting, use this bonus for the clash roll:

- Adjusted power `0-29`: `+0`
- Adjusted power `30-49`: `+1`
- Adjusted power `50-69`: `+2`
- Adjusted power `70-89`: `+3`
- Adjusted power `90-109`: `+4`
- Adjusted power `110-129`: `+5`
- Adjusted power `130+`: `+6`

#### Damage steps

1. Start with the move's printed power.
2. Apply STAB, weather, terrain, abilities, held items, stat stages, and type effectiveness to get **adjusted power**.
3. Convert adjusted power to a damage die.
4. Add the appropriate offensive combat bonus from calculated `Attack` or `Special Attack`.
5. Apply the appropriate defensive modifier from calculated `Defense` or `Special Defense`.
6. Add any remaining move text effects such as status, recharge, recoil, or self-buffs.

Damage from a successful damaging hit should not drop below `1` unless immunity, `Protect`, `Detect`, or a similar effect cancels it entirely.

Example flow:
- a physical move uses calculated `Attack` against calculated `Defense`
- a special move uses calculated `Special Attack` against calculated `Special Defense`
- if two moves collide head-on, each side can instead roll `d12 + relevant combat bonus + clash power bonus`

This keeps level differences meaningful without letting base stats flatten everything.

### Held Items

Add their effects before the defense calculation and add the effects to the move’s power. And after the final calculations, take any of their additional effects into effect (Choice items move lock, Life Orb Damage, etc)

### Multi Hit Attacks

Unless the move is specified to be 100% accurate, then it will always hit a minimum of 2 times if it hits at all. Skill Link pokemon will always hit 5 times, and Surging Strikes and Triple Dive will always hit 3 times

  

Triple Kick and Triple Axel will use this chart:

- 5-6: Hits 3 times.
    
- 3-4: Hits twice.
    
- 1-2: Hits once.
    

  

Dual Wingbeat, Dual Chop, Bonmerrang, Double Hit, Gear Grind, Double Iron Bash, Double Kick, Dragon Darts, and other specified Double Attack attacks, will always hit twice.

  

When using a Milti-Hit attack like Fury Swipes or Double Slap, Roll a D6:

- 6: 5 times
    
- 5: 4 times
    
- 4: 3 times
    
- 3: 2 times
    
- 2: 2 times
    
- 1: Miss.
    

### Speed, Turn Order and Reactionary Actions

#### Battle round structure

Each side gets the following each round:

- `1` Pokemon action
- Pokemon movement up to **half its calculated Speed in feet**
- `1` Trainer action
- Trainer movement based on Athletics
- `1` Pokemon reaction
- `1` Trainer reaction

If you are not using a battlemap, use the same structure abstractly.
The side that wins initiative resolves its Pokemon action and Trainer action first, but reactions can still interrupt if the fiction supports it.

#### Trainer actions and reactions

Common Trainer actions:

- throw a healing item or battle tool
- body-block for an ally or Pokemon
- shove, grab, or grapple a target
- strike with fists, a baton, or improvised gear
- trigger a trap, rope line, gadget, or field device
- cheer, direct, or warn a Pokemon in a way that changes positioning or timing

Common Trainer reactions:

- dodge with Athletics
- block or brace with Fortitude
- intercept an item, rope, or hazard
- warn a Pokemon and justify a better defensive line

Trainer reactions should create protection, timing, or positioning.
They should not become full extra attack turns.

#### Trainer attacks, blocks, and grapples

When a trainer directly attacks, blocks, or grapples, roll `d12 + trainer combat bonus`.

Use this trainer combat bonus table:

| Trainer stat | Combat bonus |
|---|---:|
| `5-7` | `+0` |
| `8-11` | `+1` |
| `12-15` | `+2` |
| `16-19` | `+3` |
| `20+` | `+4` |

Stat uses:

- `Strength` for punches, tackles, hauling, and grapples
- `Athletics` for throws, dodges, fast repositioning, and desperate lunges
- `Fortitude` for blocks, holds, and bracing against force
- `Intelligence` for precise gadget, trap, or device actions

Trainer damage:

- unarmed strike: `1d4`
- baton, tool, or proper melee weapon: `1d6`
- thrown improvised object: `1d4`

When a Pokemon hits a trainer, use the move's normal damage, but the trainer may spend a reaction to dodge or block.
If the trainer blocks, reduce the hit by their `Fortitude` combat bonus.
If the hit is glancing, spread, or partially covered, reduce the damage die by one step before applying that reduction.

#### Grappled

If a grapple succeeds, the target is **Grappled** until it breaks free.

While Grappled:

- movement becomes `0`
- physical attacks become clumsier and can take a DM-assigned accuracy penalty
- heavy contact attacks should usually drop one damage die step until the target escapes

Breaking free is usually `Strength` or `Athletics` against the grappler's original roll or a fresh opposed roll.

#### Speed.

Use the Pokemon's **calculated Speed**, not base Speed.

Speed decides initiative, reaction races, and how much ground the Pokemon can cover in battle.

For a direct speed contest, roll `d12 + Speed bonus` using the table above.

#### Turn Order.

The side with the higher active Pokemon Speed acts first.
If Speed is tied, use trainer Initiative as the tiebreaker.
If a move has Priority, it still jumps the line unless another Priority move contests it.

### Learning Moves and Attacks.

You are the DM, so you can decide when a pokemon learns a new move, usually during some daring or dangerous event or action when your pokemon needs to push itself to the limit, like escaping the Evil Team on a train.

## Open Battlemap Combat

If you’re using a Battlemap, then battles work differently.

### Battlemap Movement

Your trainer and Pokemon do **not** have to move in tandem.

Use these movement rules:

- the Pokemon may move up to **half its calculated Speed in feet** on its turn
- the trainer moves using the Athletics-based formula below
- the Pokemon can split its movement before and after its action if the scene supports it
- the trainer and Pokemon should usually stay within about `30 feet` of each other for clean commands, item throws, and support plays

If they split farther than that, the DM can impose a mild coordination penalty or require a clearer line of sight and command path.

#### Movement Speed

Trainer movement speed is determined by their Athletics, and the formula goes like this:

- Each square is 5 feet. You can move 1 foot for each point in athletics. So, if you have 15 athletics, you can move three squares. However, if you have 9 athletics, you can still move 2 squares, as it rounds up.

- Pokemon use half their calculated Speed in feet. Convert that to squares by dividing by `5` and rounding down, minimum `1` square if they can move at all.
    

## Battlemap Turn Order

The side with the faster active Pokemon goes first.
If the active Pokemon are tied in Speed, use trainer Initiative as the tiebreaker.

### Initiative

Based on your trainer’s Intelligence and Athletics. Add both stats together and divide by two. So: (INT + ATH)/2

## Battlemap Attacks, Effects and general Moves.

Move Power, Attacking, and Defending stay the same as always, and the math is consistent.

### Attack Area Coverage, Multi, Single, Melee, Ranged, Area Denial and Field Target Attacks

Specific attacks have different area coverage depending on what kind of move it is. All moves require line of sight, unless they cannot miss. Example: Swift.

### Single Target

#### Melee Contact

These are the base, single target attacks. They hit on the square in front of the pokemon, and deal appropriate damage. Example: Iron Head, Fire Punch, Poison Jab.

#### Ranged Single Target

These attacks can hit from a range of 4 tiles, and are usually non-contact physical attacks or special moves. Example: Vine Whip, Psycho Cut, Earth Power.

#### Long Ranged Single-Target

These attacks can hit from a very far range of 6 tiles, and only hit one singular target. These are ordinarily special moves or non-contact physical moves. Example: Thunderbolt, Psychic, Icicle Crash.

#### Single Target Charge Attacks

These moves require the pokemon to charge forward and get into melee range, helping with closing the distance. These are primarily priority moves, but there are others. Examples: Extreme Speed, Close Combat, Fake Out.

### Multi Target

#### Square Attacks

These moves are different. If a move hits all adjacent foes in the base game, then it will hit all adjacent tiles (Including diagonals). Examples: Earthquake, Dazzling Gleam, Explosion, Darkest Lariat.

#### Ranged Square Attacks

These are incredibly powerful moves that spawn a 3x3 damage area in a range of 4 tiles from the pokemon using the move. Example: Heat Wave, Blizzard, Draco Meteor.

#### Line Attacks

This type of attack covers FIVE squares in a line. These attacks are ordinarily Special attacks with Beam in the name, but there are others. Example: Flamethrower, Ice Beam.

#### Sweep Attacks

This type of attack are usually Tail attacks, but there are others, and cover the three squares directly in front of your pokemon (Forward and to two forward diagonals). Example: Dragon Tail, Iron Tail, Poison Tail, Leaf blade.

#### Multi Target Charge Attacks

These attacks force the pokemon to charge forward and hit everything in their way. The charge range is 4 tiles with the tiles on each side also being covered, so a 4,3 rectangle in any forward direction. Example: Double Edge, Surf, Flare Blitz, Megahorn.

### Area Denial

#### Field Hazards

Field Hazards are attacks like Stealth Rock, Spikes and Toxic Spikes. They cover a 20 foot area in whatever pattern the player likes, but they all must be adjacent and touching each other (Diagonals count).

#### Damage of Time

Attacks like Fire Spin and Whirlpool create a 2x2 field of damage that does 1/16th of the opponent’s health in damage for each turn they enter it or start their turn inside it.

### Field Attacks and Moves

These moves encompass moves like Weather Moves, Room Moves and Perish Song. If in the base game it affects the ENTIRE FIELD, then it falls into this category.

### Trainer Support Actions in TTRPG boss battles

In TTRPG-style boss fights, each trainer gets **one Support Action** on their turn in addition to commanding their active Pokemon.

Support Actions are the closest thing this ruleset has to a D&D bonus action. Use them for battlefield interaction, not full second turns.

Common Support Actions:

- deploy or trigger a prepared trap
- throw a healing item or hand gear to an ally
- mark weak terrain or call a target lane
- stabilize a device, anchor line, or hazard control panel
- recall and resend gear, rope, or support tools

Trap rule:

- A crafted trap usually costs **one Support Action** to place if the trainer already has it prepared.
- If the trap was set before the fight, it usually costs **one Support Action** to trigger it, redirect the boss into it, or exploit it.
- Traps in boss battles should usually cause **control**, **positioning**, or **openings** instead of huge raw damage.
- A boss should usually only suffer **one trap payoff per round** unless the DM wants a combo scene.

Examples:

- **Net Snare**: root the boss in place, reduce movement, or open a safe turn for repositioning.
- **Smoke Capsule**: break line of sight, buy a revive turn, or let the party relocate.
- **Flash Thread**: punish a charge lane or reveal an invisible boss tell.
- **Adhesive Bomb**: create rough terrain, pin a limb, or force the boss to waste movement.

# Shops and Money

After winning a pokemon battle with a trainer, the players gain Pokedollars. Pokedollars can be used to buy items from Pokemarts. Pretty simple.

  

Shops and stores can have any items the DM wants them to have, but a general inventory should include:

- Pokeballs
    
- Healing Items
    
- Utility (Escape Rope, Rebels, etc)
    

Shops can get more and better items as the story goes on and as milestones are achieved. For example: You beat the 3rd gym, so Ultra balls are unlocked. 

# Crafting

Crafting is based on **known recipes**, not every recipe in the campaign at once.

## Known recipe rule

- A trainer only knows recipes that match their current **research rank unlocks**.
- Recipes can also be learned from blueprints, professors, faction manuals, gym rewards, or story training.
- If the party has the materials but not the known recipe, they can still attempt a one-off improvised craft only if the DM says the build is obvious.

## Research rank unlock ladder

| Research rank | What unlocks |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Pokemon medicine, human medicine, food, lures |
| 3 | traps |
| 5 | expedition gear |
| 6 | tech tools, TM theory, TM blanks, and reading TM blueprints |
| 8 | seam / fusion gear |
| 10 | common TM etching at Pokemon Centers |
| 12 | field TM rig access |

TM note:

- TMs start entering the party's **known crafting lane** when **Tech Tools** unlock at rank 6.
- At that point the trainer can understand blanks, typed source prep, and blueprint logic.
- Actual etching is still gated by station quality until rank 10 for Centers and rank 12 for field rigs.

## Craft pacing

- During travel, the party can usually make **1 meaningful craft per short rest**.
- During a long rest, the party can usually make **2 quick crafts** or **1 meaningful craft plus 1 quick craft**.
- More than that should usually require downtime, a workshop, or a calm settlement scene.

Reference: [Crafting Quick Reference.md](Crafting%20Quick%20Reference.md), [Crafting Recipes - Core Families.md](Crafting%20Recipes%20-%20Core%20Families.md), [Crafting, Salvage & Field Fabrication.md](Crafting%2C%20Salvage%20%26%20Field%20Fabrication.md)

# Gathering

Gathering is the route-facing side of crafting.

## Basic gathering flow

After a route obstacle, scene, or half-day block, the party can usually make **one meaningful gather attempt**.

1. Pick the source: Pokemon parts, natural materials, salvage, or faction scrap.
2. Make the relevant check.
3. Roll on the matching biome or salvage table.
4. Convert the result into exact parts or MV bundles.

## Common gather checks

| Situation | Check |
|---|---|
| spotting useful material | Intelligence |
| safely harvesting a natural source | Intelligence or Fortitude |
| hauling, cutting, prying, or breaking | Strength |
| scavenging tech or salvage | Intelligence |
| climbing or crossing to reach the material | Athletics |

Reference: [Route Gathering, Salvage & Harvest Tables.md](Route%20Gathering%2C%20Salvage%20%26%20Harvest%20Tables.md)

# Resting

Field rest is mostly for **stamina**, not free HP. Resting still takes supplies like food, clean water, and shelter.

## Long rest

Long rests are overnight camp, inn, or safe-shelter scenes.

- Trainers restore **all SP**.
- Trainers regain `1d4 + Fortitude bonus` HP naturally.
- Pokemon regain **no HP naturally** outside a Pokemon Center.
- Medicine, cooked food, and field treatment can still be used during the rest.

## Short rest

Short rests are **1-2 hour breaks**: lunch, sheltering from weather, cooling off, tending wounds, or regrouping.

- Trainers and Pokemon restore **half of their SP**, rounded up.
- No one regains HP naturally during a short rest unless a trait, item, recipe, or special feature says otherwise.

## Pokemon Center recovery

A Pokemon Center, hospital-grade clinic, or equivalent League facility restores **all HP and SP** to trainers and Pokemon unless the story says otherwise.

## Medicine-first healing

- Outside a Pokemon Center, Pokemon are expected to need **potions, medicine, food effects, or treatment** to regain HP.
- Trainers also rely on medicine in the field, but they still get limited overnight natural healing.
- This is why the crafting medicine families matter: route travel should consume resources.

## Field Treatment (No Medicine)

Field treatment is emergency care without a potion, spray, or medical item.

- Takes **10 minutes** per target during a short or long rest.
- Requires basic bandages, clean water, or a field kit.
- Make an **Intelligence** check. The DM can raise the DC for severe wounds, but **DC 12** is the normal baseline.
- On a success, roll `1d20 + Intelligence bonus`. The target regains that **percentage of max HP**, to a maximum of **50% max HP** from one treatment.
- Each trainer or Pokemon can only benefit from **2 field treatment attempts between Pokemon Center visits**.
- On a failure, the attempt is spent and restores no HP.

# Confluence Drift & Biome Fractures (Travel Rule)

The Confluence can cause **biome fractures**: short stretches where the environment swaps (plains → beach, city alley → wet forest, etc.) without changing the endpoints of a route.

Use this when traveling between settlements using the route atlas. Travel is resolved in **half-day blocks**.

## Step 0: Base travel drain

Every **half-day of travel**:

- each trainer loses `1d12 SP`
- each Pokemon actively helping with scouting, hauling, route clearing, or repeated field actions loses `1d6 SP`

If the party is just walking with recalled or relaxed team members, only count the trainers and any obvious helper or mount.

## Step 1: Seam pressure check (d20)
Roll **once per half day route travel** and read the result by the route's **Status**.

- A **natural 1** is always **High Chaos**.
- The rest of the table tells you whether there is **No Variation**, **Mild Variation**, **Standard Variation**, or **Wild Variation**.

| d20 | Stable route | Rough route | Fractured route |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | High Chaos | High Chaos | High Chaos |
| 2-10 | No Variation | No Variation | No Variation |
| 11-15 | Mild Variation | Mild Variation | Mild Variation |
| 16-18 | Standard Variation | Standard Variation | Standard Variation |
| 19 | Standard Variation | Wild Variation | Wild Variation |
| 20 | Wild Variation | Wild Variation | Wild Variation |

Variation meaning by route status:
- **No Variation**: run the route as written.
- **Mild Variation**: one seam tell, one minor hazard, or a short off-biome pocket.
- **Standard Variation**: a meaningful route change, stronger obstacle, or a clear detour lane.
- **Wild Variation**: harsher terrain, route-pocket overwrite, wrong-lane arrival risk, or major seam weirdness.

## Step 2: Biome shift chance

Not every variation changes the biome, but every variation can.

If the Seam Pressure result is a variation, roll `d6`:

| Variation tier | Biome shift occurs on |
|---|---|
| Mild Variation | `1-2` |
| Standard Variation | `1-4` |
| Wild Variation | `1-5` |

If a biome shift occurs:
- first roll the route region's **splice-in biome table** from `map/Continent A - Terrain & Regions View (working).md`
- then use `routes/Continent A/Route Warp Region Tables.md` for the route-specific warped pocket, texture, and effect
- swap encounter tone, hazards, weather, and gather options to match the temporary biome pocket for that span

How **Route Status** changes what the same variation feels like:
- **Stable**: Mild is mostly flavor; Wild is one notable obstacle.
- **Rough**: Mild adds a hazard/check; Wild is harsher terrain/weather plus a real resource hit.
- **Fractured**: Mild is a noticeable micro-fracture; Wild risks loops/wrong-exits or a major biome overwrite.

## Step 3: Variation chart

If the Seam Pressure result is **Mild**, **Standard**, or **Wild**, roll the matching `d6` chart in the route file.

### Mild Variation (generic d6)

| d6 | What happens |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Seam tell only**: duplicated signs, wrong-angle shadows, or a language swap. No biome shift, but the party knows the route is unstable. |
| 2 | **Minor footing problem**: slick stone, loose ballast, drifted sand, or hanging roots. One fitting traversal check or `+2 SP`. |
| 3 | **Brief biome pocket**: a 5-10 minute off-biome strip appears. Roll the Biome Shift, then treat one encounter or gather scene as that pocket biome. |
| 4 | **Weather oddity**: fog bank, heat shimmer, cold burst, or static drizzle. Visibility or comfort changes for one scene. |
| 5 | **Lane wobble**: the route keeps the same destination but briefly changes which side path, dock, or service lane the party uses. |
| 6 | **Opportunity pocket**: a strange salvage site, rare herb patch, or wrong-biome wild sign appears if the party stops to investigate. |

### Standard Variation (generic d6)

| d6 | What happens |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Partial overwrite**: the middle of the route behaves like a different biome for one full scene. Roll the Biome Shift and apply it fully. |
| 2 | **Hazard lane**: weather, bad footing, or structural instability forces one obstacle scene before travel resumes. |
| 3 | **Encounter lane swap**: the current stretch uses the shifted biome's encounter tone or fishing/gather table instead of the baseline route one. |
| 4 | **Wrong-time route beat**: lights, traffic, tide, or signage act like the wrong hour or season. Add one navigation or Intelligence check. |
| 5 | **Arrival complication**: the party still arrives, but by the wrong gate, dock, district, or approach road. |
| 6 | **Lingering scar**: the route leaves a seam scar behind. Next travel on this route starts at Mild Variation minimum unless someone stabilizes it. |

### Wild Variation (generic d6)

| d6 | What happens |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Full segment overwrite**: one whole segment becomes the shifted biome, not just a pocket. |
| 2 | **Loop or wrong-lane risk**: the route doubles back, splits wrong, or folds over itself. One failed navigation check changes the arrival lane. |
| 3 | **Bizarre weather hit**: roll or choose one result from [Bizarre Weather & Fracture Climate.md](Bizarre%20Weather%20%26%20Fracture%20Climate.md). |
| 4 | **Set-piece hazard**: collapsed bridge, storm wall, flooded tunnel, glass shelf, lair detour, or similar scene-defining obstacle. |
| 5 | **Mixed-biome conflict**: two biomes overlap at once; use the nastiest feature of each until the party pushes through. |
| 6 | **Route signature event**: use the route file's strongest warped-pocket or fracture result as a full scene. |

### High Chaos (generic d4)

| d4 | What happens |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Impossible geometry**: cylinder road, Möbius path, mirrored corridors, or other space-bending traversal scene. |
| 2 | **World overlap**: another district, season, shoreline, cave, or ruin intrudes physically into the route. |
| 3 | **Fracture weather catastrophe**: roll or choose a major bizarre-weather event from [Bizarre Weather & Fracture Climate.md](Bizarre%20Weather%20%26%20Fracture%20Climate.md). |
| 4 | **Reality pocket**: a full pocket-dungeon, set-piece encounter, or route-wide anomaly appears, then snaps shut after the party resolves it. |

## Step 4: Apply travel strain

Route variation adds extra SP strain on top of the base half-day cost:

| Travel condition | Extra SP drain |
|---|---:|
| No Variation | `0 SP` |
| Mild Variation | `1d4 SP` |
| Standard Variation | `1d6 SP` |
| Wild Variation | `1d8 SP` |
| High Chaos trigger | `1d12 SP` |

Failed route checks should usually cost **+2 SP** from bad footing, hesitation, backtracking, or hauling. Dangerous failures can also deal `1d4 HP` from falls, cuts, burns, or strain.

## Step 5: Running out of stamina

- If a trainer or required mount reaches **0 SP** before the half-day finishes, the party only covers **half the intended distance** for that block.
- If someone actually **passes out**, travel stops and the party must camp, retreat, or solve the scene in place.
- If the party wants to push past normal limits, call for a **Fortitude DC 14** forced-march check. On success, they keep moving and still pay the SP cost. On failure, they gain no extra distance and take `1d4 HP` strain.

## Mounted travel

Mounted travel is faster on the rider, harder on the mount.

- The rider pays **half** the normal travel SP cost, rounded up.
- The mount loses `1d6 SP` and `1d4 HP` per half-day traveled this way.
- A proper riding harness, feed, road, or rest stop can reduce the mount HP strain if another rule, item, or trait says so.

## Travel party swaps

Normally, when the party is on a calm road, dock, or mapped trail, trainers can swap Pokemon through the Pokedex / storage sync during travel.

- Do this between half-day blocks, at camp, or during obvious safe pauses.
- Once a **Variation** or clear hazard corridor is active, the travel roster is effectively locked.
- During a variation, chase, fracture loop, warped pocket, or active boss scene, no casual storage swapping.
- To change Pokemon mid-variation, the party needs a safe node, camp break, Pokemon Center access, or explicit DM permission.

## What biome fractures do mechanically
Pick 1-2:
- **Extra SP cost** from footing, detours, weather, or hauling
- **A check** (INT to navigate, FTD to endure, ATH to traverse)
- **Encounter shift** for that fracture span
- **Arrival complication** like wrong gate, wrong district, or late arrival

Reference: [Route Day Procedure.md](Route%20Day%20Procedure.md), [Route Gathering, Salvage & Harvest Tables.md](Route%20Gathering%2C%20Salvage%20%26%20Harvest%20Tables.md), `map/Continent A - Route Biomes Index.md`, and `routes/Continent A/Route Warp Region Tables.md`

High Chaos Events are rare: they should be memorable and not constant.
Examples: the road becomes a **cylindrical tunnel** you can walk around; the path becomes a **Mobius strip**; a sharp line splits two biomes at once.

## Optional travel pressure upgrades

If you want even more weight on route play, add one or two of these:

- **Camp quality**: bad shelter removes the natural HP recovery from a long rest.
- **Supply clock**: each half-day burns 1 ration and 1 water unless the route says otherwise.
- **Arrival windows**: markets, ferries, and gym appointments can be missed if the party loses time.
- **Gear wear**: a dangerous travel failure can damage one rope, harness, tool, or detector instead of dealing HP.
- **Bond scenes**: once per route, require one trainer to spend 10 minutes on a Pokemon care or bonding scene or that Pokemon takes `+1 SP` on the next half-day.

## Travel bond scenes and level growth

If a trainer uses a Pokemon meaningfully during travel to solve an obstacle, scout a route, pull a wagon, carry someone, clear terrain, or otherwise help the party move, that Pokemon can get a **Bond Scene**.

Bond Scene rule:

- A Pokemon can earn **1 Bond Scene per half-day**.
- At the next short or long rest, the trainer can play out a short care, praise, training, or recovery scene with that Pokemon.
- Then roll a `d12`.

| Result | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 1-7 | no immediate level, but the scene still matters narratively |
| 8-10 | gain 1 Bond Mark |
| 11-12 | gain 1 level immediately |

Three Bond Marks convert into **1 level**. A Pokemon can gain at most **1 travel-earned level per day**.

GM reference: `map/Continent A - Route Biomes Index.md`

## Common Rolls & Examples

### Example 1 — Traversal (Route Variation + a check)
- Route Status: **Rough**
- Seam Pressure roll: **18 (Wild Variation)**
- DM calls for an **ATH** check to cross a fractured ravine: **DC 15**
- Trainer has **ATH 14**, so bonus is **+7** (14/2, round up)
- Player rolls **d12 = 9** → total **16** (success)
	- Result: you cross cleanly; pay normal SP cost for the day.
	- If you failed: you still cross, but it costs **+2 SP** and you lose time (or take a minor injury).

### Example 2 — Stabilizer Protocols (reducing collateral)
- A High Chaos event triggers (someone rolled a natural 1 on the Seam Pressure check).
- A trainer uses a stabilizer tag/device and attempts **INT**: **DC 16**
- Trainer has **INT 15** → bonus **+8**
- Player rolls **d12 = 8** → total **16** (success)
	- Result: reduce the event by one severity (shorter duration, fewer hazards/adds, or less damage).

### Example 3 — Defuse (save it)
- The fused boss is **incapacitated**.
- The party has the **Defusing Tool**.
- The DM sets the Defuse DC to **16** (Variant C) and allows **2 attempts** before it destabilizes.
- Trainer attempts **INT**:
	- INT 17 → bonus **+9**
	- Attempt 1: roll **d12 = 6** → total **15** (fail)
	- Attempt 2: roll **d12 = 10** → total **19** (success)
		- Result: the fusion separates, the base Pokémon stabilizes, and the party earns research credit.
		- If someone has **Defuse Assist**, they roll with **advantage** on the primary Defuse check.

## Raids, Boss Actions, and Defusing (player-facing)

Warped zones can spawn fused/rampaging bosses.

Boss Actions:
- Some bosses get **Boss Actions** that happen **between** player turns.
- Boss Actions are smaller than full turns (a shove, hazard pulse, quick strike, forced reposition).

When a fused/rampaging boss is beaten, you can usually choose:
- **Finish it**
- **Catch it** (if allowed)
- **Defuse it** (save it)

Defuse basics:
- The target must be **incapacitated**.
- You must have the **Defusing Tool**.
- You typically get **2 attempts** (INT check) before it destabilizes.

# Trainer Traits

Traits trainers can take upon leveling up:

  

- Pitcher (8 STR): Can throw a Pokeball before the first turn of battle even starts at a 1.5x catch rate. Does not stack with Quick Balls and is overridden by the 4x Multiplier. Stack multiplicatively with any other Pokeball Catching Multiplier.
    
- Black Belt (15 STR): Can use Strength stat for Athletics rolls
    
- Runner (10 ATH): Can run away from any battle, regardless of location, status or otherwise. The only thing the player cannot run from is a trainer battle.
    
- Sneak attack (10 ATH): Gains the “Ambush” ability. Can sneak attack a pokemon to get a free first hit. This only applies if they haven’t been spotted and are actively sneaking.
    
- Fisher (10 ATH): Can use a fishing rod without having to make an athletics check.
    
- Chef (10 INT): During a short rest, if you cook and spend extra supplies, the whole party restores **full SP** instead of half. Each trainer also regains `1d8 HP`, and each Pokemon in the current team regains `1/16` of its max HP, rounded up. This can only trigger once per short rest.
    
- Scholar (10 INT): Gain advantage on Intelligence checks involving history, pokemon information, and current events.
    
- Double Battler (10 INT): Can use two pokemon when dealing with Wild Encounters or Wild Trainer battles. Gym Rules are set in stone.
    
- Jr. Professor (D12 INT): When you use **Intelligence** for a Wild Bond, you gain advantage. If you meet the Respect DC but miss the Straight-catch DC, increase your final Bond Bonus by `+1`.
    
- Nurse (D12 INT): Medicine has **double effective uses** when you apply it: split one item into two normal doses, or spend one dose for double healing. You gain advantage on crafting medicine and on **Field Treatment** checks. Also gives advantage on Wild Bond attempts when dealing with an injured pokemon.
    
- Aura Guardian (10 INT): Can loosely speak with pokemon that share your type enthusiast. If you are a Wild Card, you can speak fluently with your starter pokemon.
    
- Trace (12 INT + Aura Guardian): Can copy the ability of one of your pokemon. So, if you have a pokemon with Torrent, you become more proficient in Water-Based actions when you are on low HP. Once you decide which ability to copy, it cannot be changed without removing it by psychic means.
    
- Psychic (20 INT): You can use a high DC Intelligence check to know what pokemon your battle opponent is going to send out next and give you the ability to switch.
    
- Ace Trainer (10 FTD): The more pokemon you have in your party (Max of 6) +1 bonus to the roll when catching pokemon by any means, but only during the actual attempt to catch the pokemon, not any sort of buildup.
    
- Biker (10 FTD): Can ride a bike to expend a third as much SP while traveling. (Only on flatter and mostly unobstructed terrain).
    
- Hiker (13 FTD): Expend half as much SP when going from place to place.
    
- It’s the Vibes (8 SCH): Have a 1.5x modifier to catching Pokemon of the same Gender (you know what I mean) as you
    
- Contest Star (10 SCH): Can use the Contest attributes of a Pokemon moves (Smart, Cool, Tough, Cute, Beauty) To gain bonuses on Speech rolls
    
- Down Brock (15 SCH): You can smooth talk the opposite gender very well, unless you have a pokemon that is done with your shit (The DM).
    
- Actor/Actress (15 SCH): Have a 1.5x Bonus to Speech checks while impersonating someone else or attempting to deceive someone.
    
- Test Your Luck (10 LCK): Once a day, if you fail a roll or check, you can choose to reroll it using Luck instead.
    
- Prepared For Everything (20 LCK + Test Your Luck): You can make a ‘Test Your Luck’ roll to just so happen to have exactly what you need for this specific situation. This can apply to literally anything.
    
- Guys Type trainer (No requirements): Boosts catch rate by 1.5x if a pokemon has more than one head/being in it 
    
- Expanding Horizons (No Requirements):  If you are a Wild Card, you can use this to gain a Type Specialty. If you already have a Type Specialty, then you can choose this to gain another one. This Trait has no limit, you can have as many specialties as you want. It may be a stupid idea to have so many, but you’re playing a Pokemon TTRPG, so go nuts.
    
- In Tune (Requires Type Specialty): Become in tune with your Type Specialty. If you are a Rock Type trainer, you gain bonuses to checks involving Geology or Rocks. If you’re a Water Type trainer, you gain bonuses to aquatic actions. If you’re a Fire Type trainer, you gain resistance to hot temperatures. If you’re a Normal Type trainer, you gain bonuses on basic and normal actions. Etc.

- Field Researcher (10 INT): When you’re collecting samples, running surveys, or doing on-route science, gain advantage on the relevant Intelligence checks.

- Zone Sense (12 INT): You can always attempt an INT check to “read” a route fracture (where the seam is pulling, which exit is real, what’s bait). On success, gain advantage on the next navigation/seam-read check on that route.

- Bond Whisperer (12 INT + Type Specialty): You have a strong read on Pokémon that match your Type Specialty (your “type enthusiast” lane). Gain advantage on attempts to calm, approach, or negotiate with those Pokémon.

- Gadgeteer (12 INT): You can build/repair small field gadgets during rests (detectors, lures, stabilizer tags). The DM sets the materials needed; on a success you gain a small, practical utility item for the next travel/battle scene.

- Iron Will (12 FTD): You can push through one extra training/travel scene when you should be done. You still get the benefit, but you **pass out** immediately after (you can’t take actions until the next rest/scene transition).

- Battle Analyst (12 INT): Once per battle, you can make an INT check to call the fight. On success, you learn a meaningful tactical truth (best target, likely switch, hidden trick, weakness angle) and an ally gains advantage on one related roll this round.

- Quick Hands (10 ATH): Once per battle, you can use an item (or throw a Poké Ball) without losing your action (DM timing). Doesn’t stack with other “free item” effects.

- Type Specialist (Requires Type Specialty): Your Type Specialty is more than vibes. Gain advantage on checks to identify, track, and work with Pokémon matching your specialty, and gain a 1.5x catch-rate modifier against Pokémon matching your specialty.
    
- People person (12 SCH): Instantly know when an insight (INT) roll can be used instead of having to initiate it yourself.

- Seam Cartographer (12 INT + Zone Sense): When you travel a route you’ve already mapped, you can reroll one navigation/seam-read check per travel and keep the better result.

- Fracture Runner (12 ATH): When a route becomes a traversal scene (loop risk / wrong-exit / hazard corridor), you can take point and grant the party +1 on the next traversal-related check.

- Stormwise (12 FTD): Gain advantage on checks to endure severe weather. Once per travel, you can reduce the SP cost of one weather hazard by 1 for the party (minimum 0).

- Supply Discipline (10 FTD): Once per travel day, ignore the first “extra supplies” consequence from a failed travel check (you still lose time).

- Field Lab Kit (12 INT + Field Researcher): During a rest you can process samples and bank 1 “Research Note.” Spend a Note to gain advantage on a related knowledge check later (biology, seams, materials).

- Stabilizer Protocols (15 INT): When a High Chaos or fusion event hits, you can attempt an INT check to reduce collateral.
	- On success: reduce one severity (less damage, shorter duration, fewer adds).

- Defuse Assist (12 INT + Gadgeteer): When a fused/rampaging boss is incapacitated and the party attempts a Defuse, you gain advantage on the primary Defuse INT check.

- Raid Captain (12 SCH): Boss battles only. Once per boss battle, you can issue a callout.
	- Effect: one ally gains advantage on their next attack roll/check OR can reposition without provoking (DM timing).

- Tempo Reader (12 INT + Battle Analyst): After the first full round, you can call the enemy’s pattern.
	- Effect: learn one of: its strongest move category, its main weakness angle, or what it will likely do next.

- Emergency Swap (12 ATH): Once per battle, you can switch your active Pokémon without losing your action (still respects any battle system limits).

- Calm Voice (12 SCH): Gain advantage on checks to de-escalate with wild Pokémon and prevent fights.

- Quick Fix (12 INT + Gadgeteer): Once per rest, you can repair one broken item/gear tag without needing a shop.

- Permit Forger (12 INT + Actor/Actress): Gain advantage when using paperwork, disguises, or permits to bypass checkpoints.

- Lockpick & Latch (12 ATH + Quick Hands): Gain advantage on checks to open stuck doors, hatches, lockers, and service panels without making a scene.

- Dual Specialty (15 INT + Expanding Horizons): Choose a second Type Specialty.
	- Your “outsider penalty” applies only to types outside both.
	- If a Pokémon matches either specialty, you get your normal specialty bonus.
	- If a Pokémon matches both (dual-typed), treat it as a double bonus for your specialty effects (double your catch modifier and any check bonuses tied to specialty).
	- If a Pokémon matches both types in ttrpg style battles they gain a 1.25x bonus to hp and battle bonding with them is 0.75x easier

- Terrain Attunement (Requires Type Specialty): When in a biome strongly matching your specialty, once per travel you can reduce the SP cost of one obstacle by 1 (minimum 0).
