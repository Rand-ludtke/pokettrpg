# Pokemon Contest & Showcase Draft

See also: [Main rules.md](Main%20rules.md), [Pokemon Field Stats & Skill Checks.md](Pokemon%20Field%20Stats%20%26%20Skill%20Checks.md), [Nimbasa City.md](../towns/Nimbasa%20City.md), [Nimbasa City - Places & Prices.md](../towns/Location%20Guides/Nimbasa%20City%20-%20Places%20%26%20Prices.md), [Lumiose-Levincia Quarter.md](../towns/Lumiose-Levincia%20Quarter.md), [Lumiose-Levincia Quarter - Places & Prices.md](../towns/Location%20Guides/Lumiose-Levincia%20Quarter%20-%20Places%20%26%20Prices.md), [Leader - Celeste.md](../gym%20leaders/Leader%20-%20Celeste.md)

## Core read
This draft is built to feel like the anime contest format.

Each contestant uses one Pokemon in two stages:

1. **Round 1: Appeal Stage**
2. **Round 2: Contest Battle Stage**

The point is not raw damage.
The point is presentation, timing, creativity, coordination, and whether the Pokemon looks like it belongs on a stage.

Use it for:
- ribbon-style contest days
- Lumiose-Levincia formal showcase heats
- sponsor auditions
- rival scenes where style matters as much as combat

## Default event format
- 8 contestants enter
- all 8 do Round 1
- the top 4 advance to Round 2
- Round 2 is a single-elimination mini bracket: 2 semifinals, then 1 final
- by default, the same Pokemon is used in both rounds
- casual local events can allow a different Pokemon in Round 2, but formal contests usually do not

## Contest category flavors
Keep the classic five move flavors from [Main rules.md](Main%20rules.md):
- Cute
- Beauty
- Cool
- Tough
- Smart

Use **Entrance** as the sixth scoring lane.

That gives a clean **30-point Appeal Stage**:
- Entrance: `0 to 5`
- Cute: `0 to 5`
- Beauty: `0 to 5`
- Cool: `0 to 5`
- Tough: `0 to 5`
- Smart: `0 to 5`

## What a contestant brings
Each contestant prepares:
- 1 Pokemon
- up to 4 moves they plan to feature
- 1 entrance concept
- 1 outfit, prop, or visual theme if they want the extra polish

The entrance does not need to be a move.
It can be a Ball Capsule burst, a practiced throw, a costume reveal, a stage pose, or a trainer-and-Pokemon opening beat.

## Judge panel
Use a 3-person panel by default.

Default anime panel:
- **Mr. Contesta** from the organizing committee: older, formal, experienced, and the most procedural voice on the panel
- **Mr. Sukizo** from the Pokemon Fan Club: enthusiastic, theatrical, and absolutely not old even if he acts like he invented taste
- **Local Nurse Joy** from the nearest Pokemon Center: calm, practical, and usually the cleanest judge of trainer-Pokemon coordination

At the table, the DM can run all 3 judges alone.
If you want more texture, let a third person at the table act as the crowd or judge read and help classify moments as minor, solid, or major wins and losses.

## Round 1: Appeal Stage
Round 1 is a short routine where the coordinator shows off the Pokemon's style, control, and charm.

The performance should include:
- 1 entrance moment
- up to 4 featured moves
- trainer direction and stage presence

### Running Round 1 fast
Use this sequence:

1. The player describes the routine concept in plain language.
2. The player names the entrance and up to 4 moves.
3. The player makes 1 main check.
4. The DM scores the six lanes for a maximum of 30.

### Main Round 1 check
Use `Speech` by default.

If the table wants the **Pokemon itself** to be the primary performer for a beat instead of the trainer, use `Charm` from [Pokemon Field Stats & Skill Checks.md](Pokemon%20Field%20Stats%20%26%20Skill%20Checks.md) as the Pokemon-side equivalent of `Speech`.

Why `Speech`:
- it covers command presence
- it covers how well the trainer sells the plan
- it fits the existing `Contest Star` trait in [Main rules.md](Main%20rules.md)

Use one of these if the routine clearly leans another way:
- `Speech` for commanding, emceeing, and selling the act
- `Intelligence` for tightly engineered choreography, prop timing, or move sequencing
- `Athletics` for physically active routines with catching, dancing, or precise prop work

If the table wants a second check, allow one support check:
- `Intelligence` for planning
- `Pokemon handling` for control
- `Style Kit or Grooming Kit` use for appearance-first routines

On a strong support check, add `+2` total Appeal points.
On a failed support check, subtract `1` total Appeal point if the mistake was visible.

### Contest Star trait
The `Contest Star` trait should matter here.

If the trainer has it, let them gain:
- `+1` on the Round 1 main check if at least 2 used moves match the routine's intended flavor
- `+2` instead if 3 or more used moves clearly build the same flavor lane or a clean combo chain

## Round 1 scoring lanes

### 1) Entrance `0 to 5`
This is the throw-out, arrival, first pose, or first visual impression.

Score guide:
- `0`: awkward entrance, bad timing, obvious stumble
- `1`: gets on stage but does nothing memorable
- `2`: clean entrance, reads fine, no spark
- `3`: crowd notices immediately
- `4`: polished, thematic, feels prepared
- `5`: the whole room locks in on the opening

Give advantage in spirit or a `+1 to +2` score bump if:
- Ball Capsule effects fit the Pokemon perfectly
- the throw and release were rehearsed
- the trainer outfit and Pokemon presentation feel coordinated

Subtract `1 to 2` if:
- the release is clumsy
- the Ball Capsule burst overwhelms the Pokemon instead of framing it
- the Pokemon visibly hates the outfit or prop

### 2) Cute `0 to 5`
Use for charm, sweetness, playful energy, or emotional warmth.

### 3) Beauty `0 to 5`
Use for elegance, grace, poise, polish, and visual balance.

### 4) Cool `0 to 5`
Use for confidence, sharp timing, daring lines, and commanding stage presence.

### 5) Tough `0 to 5`
Use for resilience, force, solidity, and the sense that the Pokemon owns the stage physically.

### 6) Smart `0 to 5`
Use for clever sequencing, illusions, feints, precision tricks, or technical control.

### Lane scoring rubric
Each lane should be judged by the same questions:
- did the routine actually show this flavor
- was the Pokemon in control
- did the move choice fit the visual idea
- did the act feel rehearsed without feeling dead

Quick lane rubric:
- `0`: absent or actively undermined
- `1`: barely present
- `2`: present but weak
- `3`: solid and readable
- `4`: strong and memorable
- `5`: standout lane of the routine

## Round 1 bonuses and penalties

### Good reasons to add points
- strong move combination that clearly pays off from setup to finish
- Ball Capsule burst that frames the first move instead of distracting from it
- stage props used intelligently
- Pokemon personality comes through
- the final move recontextualizes the earlier routine in a smart way

### Good reasons to trim points
- same move visual repeated without escalation
- pretty idea, weak execution
- overcomplicated routine that the Pokemon cannot actually perform cleanly
- trainer speech says one thing, stage picture shows another
- the Pokemon looks confused, bored, or stressed

### Repetition rule
Borrow the anime and game logic that repeating the same appeal too obviously gets stale.

If the same move is used in Round 1 in nearly the same way twice:
- subtract `1` from the weaker lane it was trying to score
- ignore this if the repeat is part of a clear setup-payoff combination

## Advancing to Round 2
After all Appeal Stage scores are set:
- rank contestants by total Appeal score out of 30
- the top 4 advance
- ties are broken by higher Entrance score, then crowd reaction, then judge decision

If you want a quick crowd tiebreaker, have tied contestants each make one final `Speech` or `Composure` check and advance the cleaner closer.

## Round 2: Contest Battle Stage
Round 2 should feel like an anime contest battle, not a normal knockout fight.

Use the normal battle map, ranges, and speed rules from your TTRPG.
Ignore HP for win condition.
Track **Appeal Points** instead.

### Contest Battle setup
- both contestants start at `60 Appeal Points`
- the battle lasts `5 turns`
- if one side hits `0`, faints, or is unable to continue, they lose immediately
- if both are still standing after turn 5, the higher Appeal Point total wins
- the faster Pokemon gets the **tempo advantage** on turns `1, 3, and 5`
- on a tempo-advantage turn, that Pokemon may change its **approach** after declarations are heard, but not the declared move unless it spends a reaction

This keeps speed relevant without forcing the faster side to blindly act first every time.

### Same-Pokemon expectation
Formal contests usually expect the same Pokemon from Round 1 to continue into Round 2.

That matters because:
- it rewards preparation instead of last-second hard counters
- it makes the Pokemon feel like the actual star of the event
- it matches the anime contest feel better

## Contest battle stats
Round 2 uses the same five Pokemon-facing scene stats as your field-stat rules, but they are based on the Pokemon's **calculated battle stats**, not base stats.

Use the same type-bonus map as [Pokemon Field Stats & Skill Checks.md](Pokemon%20Field%20Stats%20%26%20Skill%20Checks.md).

```text
ceil10(x) = ceil(x / 10)
ceil20(x) = ceil(x / 20)
clampStat(x) = min(20, max(3, x))
typeBonus(stat, types) = number of the Pokemon's types that match that stat, max 2
contestBonus(stat) = ceil(stat / 2)
```

Then calculate:

```text
Strength = clampStat(ceil10(calculated Attack) + typeBonus(Strength, types))
Athletics = clampStat(ceil10(calculated Speed) + typeBonus(Athletics, types))
Intelligence = clampStat(ceil20(calculated Sp. Atk + calculated Sp. Def) + typeBonus(Intelligence, types))
Fortitude = clampStat(ceil20(calculated HP + calculated Defense) + typeBonus(Fortitude, types))
Charm = clampStat(ceil20(calculated HP + calculated Sp. Def) + typeBonus(Charm, types))
```

Round 2 contested rolls use:

```text
contest roll = d12 + contestBonus(relevant stat) + movePowerBonus
```

## Round 2 turn structure
Each Pokemon gets:

- `1` declared move each turn
- free stage reposition up to **half its calculated Speed in feet**
- up to `2` total reactions for the entire battle
- never more than `1` reaction in the same turn

The contest should feel like the judges and crowd can read what is coming, even when they do not know the full payoff yet.

### Partial declaration
At the start of each turn, both sides declare:

- the move they are preparing
- the broad approach they are taking with it

Approach examples:

- attack
- block
- dodge
- setup
- reposition
- combo

The DM should then say what is visibly readable on stage.

Examples:

- "They seem to be building a defensive wall with Ancient Power."
- "They look like they are preparing to crash in on a large Surf wave."
- "That stance looks like a dodge-and-counter line, not a straight hit."

On turns `1, 3, and 5`, the faster Pokemon may change its **approach** after declarations are heard, but it may not change the declared move for free.

## Reaction uses
A reaction in Round 2 can only be spent in one of these two ways:

### 1) Change move reaction
Spend `1` reaction after declarations to switch the declared move.

This lets a slower Pokemon adapt even when it did not win the speed read.

If both sides spend a reaction to change move on the same turn:

- the slower side commits first
- the faster side commits second

### 2) Combo attack reaction
Spend `1` reaction to turn the turn into a two-move sequence.

When declaring a combo:

- reveal only the **first** move and the broad approach
- keep the second move hidden until resolution

If the combo succeeds cleanly, increase the Appeal loss by one band.
If it fails, the acting Pokemon usually suffers the next worse loss band itself.

## Resolving the exchange
Use this order each turn:

1. Both sides declare move plus approach.
2. The DM states the obvious stage read.
3. On turns `1, 3, and 5`, the faster side may change its approach.
4. Either side may spend a reaction to change move or declare a combo.
5. Both Pokemon reposition up to half Speed.
6. Roll the opposed contest check.
7. Apply Appeal loss, status fallout, or knockout consequences.

## Picking the relevant stat
Use the stat that best matches **how** the move is being used, not only the move's normal category.

Quick picks:

- **Strength**: overpowering, charging, slamming through, direct physical collision
- **Athletics**: dodging, weaving, skimming, rapid reposition, evasive timing
- **Intelligence**: beam control, barriers, shaping, precision, baiting angles, setup work
- **Fortitude**: bracing, shielding, weathering force, holding a line, enduring a crash
- **Charm**: feints, posture, crowd-selling, confidence beats, pressure that wins the room

Common matchups:

- attack into block: `Strength` vs `Fortitude`
- wave, beam, or ranged pressure into a sidestep: `Intelligence` or `Strength` vs `Athletics`
- setup move trying to outfox a rush: `Intelligence` vs `Athletics`
- defensive screen or Ancient Power wall into a breaking strike: `Fortitude` or `Intelligence` vs `Strength`

## Move power bonus
Use the move's adjusted power after normal modifiers.

- adjusted power `0-29`: `+0`
- adjusted power `30-49`: `+1`
- adjusted power `50-69`: `+2`
- adjusted power `70-89`: `+3`
- adjusted power `90-109`: `+4`
- adjusted power `110-129`: `+5`
- adjusted power `130+`: `+6`

This is what keeps weak moves from bullying strong moves without a very good reason.

## Head-on move clashes
If both sides are directly trying to beat the other move head-on, use the normal opposed contest roll, but treat it as a true clash.

If one move would be **super effective** against the opposing Pokemon, multiply that move's **move power bonus** by `1.5`, rounded down, for the clash.

This only changes the clash roll, not the rest of your damage rules elsewhere.

## How Appeal Points are lost
Do not calculate HP damage into Appeal Point loss.
Judge the exchange by how strong, clean, risky, and impressive it looked.

Use this loss scale:

- **Minor loss:** `1d4`
- **Solid loss:** `2d6`
- **Major loss:** `3d6`
- **Reversal or spectacular counter:** `4d6`

### When to assign Appeal loss
Cause the opponent to lose Appeal when:

- you win the exchange cleanly and it reads well on stage
- you block or counter in a way that makes them look flat
- your setup pays off and steals the room
- they overextend, stumble, or reveal the wrong line badly
- your combo lands and the hidden second move reframes the whole turn

Cause the acting Pokemon to lose Appeal when:

- it misses cleanly and everyone sees it
- it spends a reaction badly and gets blown up for it
- it tries to force a combo that never comes together
- it repeats a stale look with no escalation

## Round 2 result guide

### Minor loss `1d4`
Use when:

- the move worked but looked only fine
- the dodge avoided trouble without turning the moment
- the stumble was small

### Solid loss `2d6`
Use when:

- a clear hit or clean defense wins the beat
- the opponent reveals the wrong line and gets outplayed
- the crowd clearly shifts but momentum does not fully break

### Major loss `3d6`
Use when:

- a direct clash is won decisively
- a counter completely stuffs the opponent's idea
- a strong setup payoff lands exactly as planned
- the loser looks rattled, messy, or outclassed in public

### Reversal `4d6`
Use sparingly.
This is for moments like:

- the second move of a combo completely flips the exchange
- a dodge or block turns into a full public humiliation
- one Pokemon uses the opponent's momentum as the centerpiece of its own finish

## Fainting and incapacitation
If a Pokemon faints in Round 2, it loses immediately.

If a Pokemon is asleep, frozen solid, or otherwise unable to act when its move would resolve:

- it immediately suffers `3d6` Appeal loss
- if it is still unable to act at the start of the next turn, the judges usually stop the match there

That gives status and knockout moments real weight instead of making them feel cosmetic.

## Round 2 style calls
Use these calls to keep the battle moving.

### Stylish hit
The move hits and looks good.
Opponent loses Appeal Points.

### Stylish counter
The move blocks, redirects, or reframes the opponent's move.
Opponent loses more Appeal Points than they normally would from a standard hit.

### Stylish defense
The Pokemon protected itself well and still looked composed.
Reduce incoming loss by one step:
- major becomes solid
- solid becomes minor
- minor becomes none

### Stylish dodge
The Pokemon avoids the hit cleanly and turns the miss into part of its own image.
Usually cancel the incoming loss, then deal **minor** or **solid** loss back.
If the dodge completely humiliates the attacker, treat it as **major**.

### Miss or stumble
If the Pokemon misses or loses stage control in an obvious way, it loses Appeal Points itself.

### Flat success
The move worked but did not impress.
Treat as minor loss or no point swing.

## Combo rule
Borrow the contest logic that combinations matter.

If one move clearly sets up the next move in a readable way, the payoff move gains one step of severity:
- minor to solid
- solid to major
- major to reversal if it came from a declared combo reaction and the table agrees it was match-defining

Examples:
- mist into light beam
- sand veil into silhouette reveal
- defense curl into elegant rollout finish
- protect into sparkling close-range counter

If the combo was only possible because of a spent reaction, it should hit harder on success but punish harder on failure.

## Repetition penalty
If a contestant uses the same move on back-to-back turns with no real change in presentation:
- they lose `1d4` Appeal Points
- the crowd stops buying the trick

Ignore this penalty if:
- the move is a deliberate escalating signature sequence
- the second use pays off a setup from the first use

## Defensive and support moves in contest battles
Protective or setup moves should still matter.

Use them like this:
- `Protect`, `Detect`, or equivalent can justify `Fortitude` contests, reduce incoming Appeal loss, or set up a stylish counter window
- dance, stance, or charge moves can grant `+1 severity step` to the next clean payoff
- illusion, mist, screen, terrain, weather, or displacement moves can shift the relevant contest stat toward `Intelligence` or `Charm`
- mobility moves can justify a stronger `Athletics` contest and make the next enemy miss count as a more severe stumble

This keeps support Pokemon relevant instead of forcing pure attack spam.

## Judge role during Round 2
Have the judge panel comment on exchanges instead of silently scoring every beat.

Good judge prompts:
- "That was clean, but not special."
- "Strong recovery."
- "They stole your moment there."
- "Great control."
- "The crowd loved that counter."

If you want more table help, assign one person to call the loss band before the die is rolled:
- minor
- solid
- major

## Crowd meter optional rule
If you want more anime energy, add a crowd meter from `-2` to `+2`.

- start at `0`
- especially flashy moments raise it by `1`
- awkward misses or boring repeats lower it by `1`

At `+2`:
- the next strong combo or stylish counter gets `+1d6` extra Appeal loss

At `-2`:
- even a clean hit only counts as minor unless it truly resets the room

## Quick prep rules before a contest
Let players prepare in ways that matter.

### Capsule and sticker prep
Players can buy:
- Ball Capsules
- seal or sticker packs
- costume pieces
- grooming supplies
- rehearsal time

Training the entrance matters.

Before a contest, a trainer can spend one prep scene practicing the throw and first pose.
Make one check:
- `Speech` for command timing
- `Athletics` for the physical throw and catch rhythm
- `Intelligence` for sequencing capsule effects and stage marks

On success, gain one of:
- `+1` Entrance score in Round 1
- ignore one small entrance stumble
- advantage in spirit on the first judge read for the routine

On a strong success, also let the Pokemon stay visibly composed after release even in a loud arena.

### Routine rehearsal
A trainer can also rehearse one move combination before the event.

Use:
- `Intelligence` to plan it
- `Speech` to drill it
- `Fortitude` if the routine is physically punishing or takes repeated tries

On success, the first time that combo appears in Round 1 or 2, treat it as one step cleaner than it would otherwise be.

## One-scene quick version
If you do not want the full bracket, compress it:

1. one Round 1 Appeal score out of 30
2. one Round 2 contest battle scene using 3 turns instead of 5

Use this for:
- side events
- travel detours
- quick rival scenes

## Design notes
This draft is trying to keep three things true at once:
- anime-style two-stage contests
- your existing stat system and battle timing
- enough structure that the DM is not forced to improvise every point swing from nothing

That means you should judge for spectacle first, but still use the point-loss bands so the battle has actual momentum.
