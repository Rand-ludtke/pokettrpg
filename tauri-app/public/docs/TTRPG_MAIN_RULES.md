# Stats

You have 20 points to allocate to each stat, each stat has a base of 5.

How ability checks work is D12 + stat/2 (Round up). Stats grant a bonus to rolls, but DCs are quite high. (15-25) On Maximum roll (Natural 12) double the stat value. So, with ten strength, a natural 12 will be a 22. (12 +2(10/2)). Divide the stat by two and round up.

Each player starts with a trait (see Trainer Traits).

## Strength

- General physical strength actions: Lifting things, throwing things, attacking, etc.

Strength is used in a variety of scenarios, but mostly doing that might help with traversal or when you need to force your way through something. If a wild pokemon is giving you trouble, nothing is stopping you from just hitting it with a stick and calling it a day.

## Athletics

- General athletic actions: Running, jumping, movement based actions.
- Effects dodge chance. (D12 + stat/2 (Round up))
- Effects Base Stamina (SP) (stat + 5)

Stamina determines how far you can travel in a single day without resting, and determines how long you can train without a trip back to a Pokémon Center. So, if you are attempting to travel between towns that have a mountain separating them, then it will take more stamina than going between towns with a single route between them. If you are having an intensive training session, you can go even harder with higher SP. So having low stamina will result in needing to rest more often and use more supplies.

Stamina can only be restored via short or long rest.

## Intelligence

- General decision making, investigation, and “reading the room” actions.
- Effects knowledge checks (Checks to get information that the player either cannot remember or that a well-read character might know) (D12 + stat/2)
- Perception checks (Seeing something that might otherwise be hidden)
- Insight checks (seeing if someone is lying, hiding something, or reading their general mood)

Intelligence is based on the mind and memory. Intelligence can be used to get pokemon type information, weaknesses and potential moves that they may know in battle. It can be used to see if a character they are talking to is lying, or to see if information they give may or may not be correct. It can also be used to check the history of an event or a place that might be important to the story or what you’re doing.

## Speech

- Social checks: persuasion, deception, intimidation, negotiation.
- Performance and presentation.

## Fortitude

- Endurance and willpower under stress.
- Resisting exhaustion, pain, fear, and harsh environments.
- Used for pushing training/travel beyond normal limits.

## Luck

Luck is used for finding specific pokemon you want and catching them with ease. This stat allows for some fun antics for you to get up to and come out unscathed, and allow for “Test Your Luck” rolls.

# Battle Bond (Trait draft)

Battle Bond is treated as a Trait that you can only get through the Fusion Machine / Bond Sync setup.

Baseline idea:
- It is temporary (short buff window), not a permanent mega-stat rewrite.
- It should feel strong, but come with a clear cost afterward.

Effect (draft):
- +1 stage to one offensive stat and +1 stage Speed.

Requirements (draft):
- Trainer must meet a Fortitude requirement: 15 (your body surviving the sync).
- Partner Pokémon must have maxed happiness.

Activation (draft):
- Once per long rest, you can run a Bond Sync on one Pokémon to trigger Battle Bond.

Cost (draft):
- While active, Battle Bond drains Stamina (SP) per turn based on the Pokémon’s level:
  - Level 50 and below: drain 1 SP/turn
  - Level 51+: drain 2 SP/turn

DM reference: Rules/DM Rules - Level Caps, Raids, Fusion, Defuse.md

# Insight Checks

For Insight checks, you roll Intelligence.

Insight is your tool for:
- reading mood (fear/anger/excitement)
- spotting lies / withholding
- reading intent (stalling, threatening, bargaining)

Insight is not mind-reading:
- it won’t reveal exact thoughts
- it won’t auto-solve mysteries without evidence

On a success, the DM gives you 1–2 clear tells and what they suggest.

# Type Enthusiast

At the time of character creation, you can choose to specialize in a specific type. It leads to the following benefits:

- You find them more often
- Have a 1.5X multiplier when catching them and with Wild Bond
- Have an understanding of pokemon of that type better giving you advantage on intelligence rolls when that type is involved.

But doing so leads to the following nerfs

- You find other types of pokemon less, leading to less type variety
- Have a 0.75X Catching Multiplier when attempting to catch or Wild Bond with pokemon outside that type.

If you choose to not specialize in a specific type, then you get the, “Wild Card” tag to mark on your sheet. You don’t get the buffs, but you also don’t get nerfs

## Pokemon Leveling

Pokemon levels can go over level 100 in this campaign.

Pokemon level from battling and by doing miscellaneous actions (Breaking rocks, getting berries from trees, etc). It’s mostly on what moves the pokemon can know, and if the DM thinks that a certain move is far too high of a level for a pokemon to know, then they have the ability to VETO it.

TMs and HMs (Technical Machines and Hidden Machines) are also in this category, as they need to be bought or found in the wild to be used. They can be of infinite or limited use, whatever the DM decides.

When directly training:

Roll a D12 and subtract that number from your SP. If you still have SP remaining you can keep rolling until you get to 0. However, points you go over your SP Max, you take that much in damage to HP. You can only regain HP when you rest. If you go over your amount of HP and pass out, the pokemon gets zero levels.

For example: If you have 13 SP, and roll a 4 on the D12. the SP becomes 9 and the Pokémon trained gains 1 level.

# Battling

Use the battling app you made.

If you want a tabletop fallback (or want it like the anime), use this:

### Pokemon HP

Take the Base HP stat of every pokemon, Divide it by 2 (Rounded down) and add 1 HP per pokemon level. This statistic does not apply to Shedinja. ((Base HP Stat / 2) + 1(Level))

### Pokemon Attacking and Defending
- Power 30-59: D6
- Power 0-29: D4

Roll the specified dice when attacking, and deal the amount of damage you rolled, it’s rather simple.

#### Defending

To determine the brackets of defence to resist damage, use this chart (This applies to both Defense and Special Defense when resisting their specific kinds of moves):

- Base Defense/Special Defense 150+: - 4
- Base Defense/Special Defense 120-149: -3
- Base Defense/Special Defense 100-119: -2
- Base Defense/Special Defense 80-99: -1
- Base Defense/Special Defense 60-79: None
- Base Defense/Special Defense 0-59: +1

When received an attack, subtract the number rolled from the final result.

So, if my Rhydon (Defence 120) is hit with a Neutral Psycho Cut from Gallade, the following will happen.
- Gallade (125 base attack) uses Psycho Cut (Power 70) plus STAB (Power 105) and since it’s neutral damage, there are no type interactions or resistances, meaning it would hit Rhydon with 1D12 of damage. With Gallade’s 125 Base attack stat, it would add a flat +4 to the damage. Now, Rhydon has a 120 base defense state, meaning it would subtract a flat 3 from the final damage.

Take the Base power of the move and add any potential modifiers (Weather, STAB, stat boosts, Type Interactions, Terrain, etc), determine the attacking stat and defensive stat of the two pokemon, and determine what kind of Dice the attack will use.

For instance, take a flamethrower from Charizard on the opposing Blastoise.

- Flamethrower (Power 90) would get a boost from STAB (Same Type Attack Boost) and would get a 1.5x modifier (Power 135), and then be halved by Blastoise's water type (Power 67.5) meaning it would be rolled with a D8 for damage. Added with Charizard’s 109 Special attack stat, it would add a flat +3 to the damage. Now, Blastoise's Special Defense is 109, meaning that it would subtract a flat 2 from the final damage.

Add and other effects on top of the move, such as status conditions, chances to miss, and recharge as needed.

### Held Items

Add their effects before the defense calculation and add the effects to the move’s power. And after the final calculations, take any of their additional effects into effect (Choice items move lock, Life Orb Damage, etc)

### Multi Hit Attacks

Unless the move is specified to be 100% accurate, then it will always hit a minimum of 2 times if it hits at all. Skill Link pokemon will always hit 5 times, and Surging Strikes and Triple Dive will always hit 3 times

Triple Kick and Triple Axel will use this chart:

- 5-6: Hits 3 times.
- 3-4: Hits twice.
- 1-2: Hits once.

Dual Wingbeat, Dual Chop, Bonmerrang, Double Hit, Gear Grind, Double Iron Bash, Double Kick, Dragon Darts, and other specified Double Attack attacks, will always hit twice.

When using a Milti-Hit attack like Fury Swipes or Double Slap, Roll a D6:

- 6: 5 times
- 5: 4 times
- 4: 3 times
- 3: 2 times
- 2: 2 times
- 1: Miss.

### Speed, Turn Order and Reactionary Actions

#### Speed.

Take your pokemon’s base speed, that is now its speed stat, deal with it. This stat will determine turn order, and if your pokemon can take any kind of reactionary action, depending on the move.

#### Turn Order.

Which pokemon is faster and has the higher speed stat, as simple as that. If your pokemon uses a Priority Move, then you go first.

### Learning Moves and Attacks.

You are the DM, so you can decide when a pokemon learns a new move, usually during some daring or dangerous event or action when your pokemon needs to push itself to the limit, like escaping the Evil Team on a train.

## Open Battlemap Combat

If you’re using a Battlemap, then battles work differently.

### Battlemap Movement

Your trainer and your pokemon move in tandem. Your Pokemon (Depending on how big it is. See Sizes) travels and moves adjacent to your trainer on any tile they wish, and this tile can be changed at any point during their turn to an upper limit of THREE TIMES PER TURN, and takes their base movement speed as they travel together. Your pokemon may move up to one space away from their trainer to use a move or attack, but they must end their turn within the adjacent tiles of their trainer.

#### Movement Speed

Trainer movement speed is determined by their Athletics, and the formula goes like this:

- Each square is 5 feet. You can move 1 foot for each point in athletics. So, if you have 15 athletics, you can move three squares. However, if you have 9 athletics, you can still move 2 squares, as it rounds up.
- Pokemon move with their trainer, so their speed doesn’t apply to their movement.

## Battlemap Turn Order

Pokemon with the highest speed PLUS trainer Initiative move first. See Initiative.

### Initiative

Based on your trainer’s Intelligence and Athletics. Add both stats together and divide by two. So: (INT + ATH)/2

## Battlemap Attacks, Effects and general Moves.

Move Power, Attacking, and Defending stay the same as always, and the math is consistent.

### Attack Area Coverage, Multi, Single, Melee, Ranged, Area Denial and Field Target Attacks,

Specific attacks have different area coverage depending on what kind of move it is. All moves require line of sight, unless they cannot miss. Example: Swift.

### Single Target

#### Melee Contact

These are the base, single target attacks. They hit on the square in front of the pokemon, and deal appropriate damage. Example: Iron Head, Fire Punch, Poison Jab.

#### Ranged Single Target

These attacks can hit from a range of 4 tiles, and are usually non-contact physical attacks or special moves. Example: Vine Whip, Psycho Cut, Earth Power.

#### Long Ranged Single-Target

These attacks can hit from a very far range of 6 tiles, and only hit one singular target. These are ordinarily special moves or non-contact physical moves. Example: Thunderbolt, Psychic, Icicle Crash.

#### Single Target Charge Attacks

These moves require the pokemon to charge forward and get into melee range, helping with closing the distance. These are primarily priority moves, but there are others. Examples: Extreme Speed, Close Combat, Fake Out.

### Multi Target

#### Square Attacks

These moves are different. If a move hits all adjacent foes in the base game, then it will hit all adjacent tiles (Including diagonals). Examples: Earthquake, Dazzling Gleam, Explosion, Darkest Lariat.

#### Ranged Square Attacks

These are incredibly powerful moves that spawn a 3x3 damage area in a range of 4 tiles from the pokemon using the move. Example: Heat Wave, Blizzard, Draco Meteor.

#### Line Attacks

This type of attack covers FIVE squares in a line. These attacks are ordinarily Special attacks with Beam in the name, but there are others. Example: Flamethrower, Ice Beam.

#### Sweep Attacks

This type of attack are usually Tail attacks, but there are others, and cover the three squares directly in front of your pokemon (Forward and to two forward diagonals). Example: Dragon Tail, Iron Tail, Poison Tail, Leaf blade.

#### Multi Target Charge Attacks

These attacks force the pokemon to charge forward and hit everything in their way. The charge range is 4 tiles with the tiles on each side also being covered, so a 4,3 rectangle in any forward direction. Example: Double Edge, Surf, Flare Blitz, Megahorn.

### Area Denial

#### Field Hazards

Field Hazards are attacks like Stealth Rock, Spikes and Toxic Spikes. They cover a 20 foot area in whatever pattern the player likes, but they all must be adjacent and touching each other (Diagonals count).

#### Damage of Time

Attacks like Fire Spin and Whirlpool create a 2x2 field of damage that does 1/16th of the opponent’s health in damage for each turn they enter it or start their turn inside it.

### Field Attacks and Moves

These moves encompass moves like Weather Moves, Room Moves and Perish Song. If in the base game it affects the ENTIRE FIELD, then it falls into this category.

# Shops and Money

After winning a pokemon battle with a trainer, the players gain Pokedollars. Pokedollars can be used to buy items from Pokemarts. Pretty simple.

Shops and stores can have any items the DM wants them to have, but a general inventory should include:

- Pokeballs
- Healing Items
- Utility (Escape Rope, Rebels, etc)

Shops can get more and better items as the story goes on and as milestones are achieved. For example: You beat the 3rd gym, so Ultra balls are unlocked.

# Resting

Taking a normal rest will only restore half of your HP and SP. Resting takes supplies like food and water, which can be bought in bulk at any Pokemart.

Long rests are when a day goes by and the party camps out for the night. This restores all SP and HP of both the party and all their pokemon.

Short rests are short breaks as they travel. These can range from resting and attempting a Wild Bond to finding a cave to take refuge from an unforeseen storm. This restores half of the party’s SP, but only a quarter of their HP.

# Confluence Drift & Biome Fractures (Travel Rule)

The Confluence can cause biome fractures: short stretches where the environment swaps (plains → beach, city alley → wet forest, etc.) without changing the endpoints of a route.

Use this when traveling between settlements using the route atlas.

## Step 1: Route Variation (d6)
Roll once per route travel.
- 1–3: Mild Variation — mostly familiar; small fracture spans; fewer penalties.
- 4–5: Standard Variation — the route as expected.
- 6: Wild Variation — bigger fractures, harsher hazards, or meaningful detours that still reach the destination.

How Route Status changes what “Mild/Standard/Wild” means:
- Stable: Mild is mostly flavor; Wild is one notable obstacle.
- Rough: Mild adds a hazard/check; Wild is harsher terrain/weather plus a real resource hit.
- Fractured: Mild is a noticeable micro-fracture; Wild risks loops/wrong-exits or a major biome overwrite.

## Step 2: High Chaos Check (d20)
Roll a High Chaos check when indicated by the route’s Status.
- On a natural 1, a High Chaos Event triggers (reality-bending set piece).

When to roll the High Chaos check (by route Status):
- Stable: only on clear triggers (storm day, seam flare, plot beat).
- Rough: once per route travel.
- Fractured: once per segment, or once per hour of travel.

## What biome fractures do mechanically
Keep it lightweight; pick 1–2:
- Extra SP cost (harder footing, detours, weather)
- A check (INT to navigate, FTD to endure, ATH to traverse)
- Encounter shift (different Pokémon types show up for that span)
- Arrival complication (wrong district / wrong gate / delayed arrival)

High Chaos Events are rare: they should be memorable and not constant.
Examples: the road becomes a cylindrical tunnel you can walk around; the path becomes a Mobius strip; a sharp line splits two biomes at once.

## Common Rolls & Examples

### Example 1 — Traversal (Route Variation + a check)
- Route Status: Rough
- Route Variation roll: 6 (Wild Variation)
- DM calls for an ATH check to cross a fractured ravine: DC 15
- Trainer has ATH 14, so bonus is +7 (14/2, round up)
- Player rolls d12 = 9 → total 16 (success)
  - Result: you cross cleanly; pay normal SP cost for the day.
  - If you failed: you still cross, but it costs +2 SP and you lose time (or take a minor injury).

### Example 2 — Stabilizer Protocols (reducing collateral)
- A High Chaos event triggers (someone rolled a natural 1 on the High Chaos check).
- A trainer uses a stabilizer tag/device and attempts INT: DC 16
- Trainer has INT 15 → bonus +8
- Player rolls d12 = 8 → total 16 (success)
  - Result: reduce the event by one severity (shorter duration, fewer hazards/adds, or less damage).

### Example 3 — Defuse (save it)
- The fused boss is incapacitated.
- The party has the Defusing Tool.
- The DM sets the Defuse DC to 16 (Variant C) and allows 2 attempts before it destabilizes.
- Trainer attempts INT:
  - INT 17 → bonus +9
  - Attempt 1: roll d12 = 6 → total 15 (fail)
  - Attempt 2: roll d12 = 10 → total 19 (success)
    - Result: the fusion separates, the base Pokémon stabilizes, and the party earns research credit.
    - If someone has Defuse Assist, they roll with advantage on the primary Defuse check.

## Raids, Boss Actions, and Defusing (player-facing)

Warped zones can spawn fused/rampaging bosses.

Boss Actions:
- Some bosses get Boss Actions that happen between player turns.
- Boss Actions are smaller than full turns (a shove, hazard pulse, quick strike, forced reposition).

When a fused/rampaging boss is beaten, you can usually choose:
- Finish it
- Catch it (if allowed)
- Defuse it (save it)

Defuse basics:
- The target must be incapacitated.
- You must have the Defusing Tool.
- You typically get 2 attempts (INT check) before it destabilizes.

# Trainer Traits

Traits trainers can take upon leveling up:

- Pitcher (8 STR): Can throw a Pokeball before the first turn of battle even starts at a 1.5x catch rate. Does not stack with Quick Balls and is overridden by the 4x Multiplier. Stack multiplicatively with any other Pokeball Catching Multiplier.
- Black Belt (15 STR): Can use Strength stat for Athletics rolls
- Runner (10 ATH): Can run away from any battle, regardless of location, status or otherwise. The only thing the player cannot run from is a trainer battle.
- Sneak attack (10 ATH): Gains the “Ambush” ability. Can sneak attack a pokemon to get a free first hit. This only applies if they haven’t been spotted and are actively sneaking.
- Fisher (10 ATH): Can use a fishing rod without having to make an athletics check.
- Chef (10 INT): Can cook the gang a meal! By using extra supplies, you can restore the entire gang to full HP and SP at any time!
- Scholar (10 INT): Gain advantage on Intelligence checks involving history, pokemon information, and current events.
- Double Battler (10 INT): Can use two pokemon when dealing with Wild Encounters or Wild Trainer battles. Gym Rules are set in stone.
- Jr. Professor (D12 INT): You may use intelligence to attempt a Wild Bond.
- Nurse (D12 INT): Can heal People and Pokemon during a rest to full HP without expending any supplies. Also gives advantage on Wild Bond attempts when dealing with an injured pokemon.
- Aura Guardian (10 INT): Can loosely speak with pokemon that share your type enthusiast. If you are a Wild Card, you can speak fluently with your starter pokemon.
- Trace (12 INT + Aura Guardian): Can copy the ability of one of your pokemon. So, if you have a pokemon with Torrent, you become more proficient in Water-Based actions when you are on low HP. Once you decide which ability to copy, it cannot be changed without removing it by psychic means.
- Psychic (20 INT): You can use a high DC Intelligence check to know what pokemon your battle opponent is going to send out next and give you the ability to switch.
- Ace Trainer (10 FTD): The more pokemon you have in your party (Max of 6) +1 bonus to the roll when catching pokemon by any means, but only during the actual attempt to catch the pokemon, not any sort of buildup.
- Biker (10 FTD): Can ride a bike to expend a third as much SP while traveling. (Only on flatter and mostly unobstructed terrain).
- Hiker (13 FTD): Expend half as much SP when going from place to place.
- It’s the Vibes (8 SCH): Have a 1.5x modifier to catching Pokemon of the same Gender (you know what I mean) as you
- Contest Star (10 SCH): Can use the Contest attributes of a Pokemon moves (Smart, Cool, Tough, Cute, Beauty) To gain bonuses on Speech rolls
- Down Brock (15 SCH): You can smooth talk the opposite gender very well, unless you have a pokemon that is done with your shit (The DM).
- Actor/Actress (15 SCH): Have a 1.5x Bonus to Speech checks while impersonating someone else or attempting to deceive someone.
- Test Your Luck (10 LCK): Once a day, if you fail a roll or check, you can choose to reroll it using Luck instead.
- Prepared For Everything (20 LCK + Test Your Luck): You can make a ‘Test Your Luck’ roll to just so happen to have exactly what you need for this specific situation. This can apply to literally anything.
- Guys Type trainer (No requirements): Boosts catch rate by 1.5x if a pokemon has more than one head/being in it
- Expanding Horizons (No Requirements): If you are a Wild Card, you can use this to gain a Type Specialty. If you already have a Type Specialty, then you can choose this to gain another one. This Trait has no limit, you can have as many specialties as you want. It may be a stupid idea to have so many, but you’re playing a Pokemon TTRPG, so go nuts.
- In Tune (Requires Type Specialty): Become in tune with your Type Specialty. If you are a Rock Type trainer, you gain bonuses to checks involving Geology or Rocks. If you’re a Water Type trainer, you gain bonuses to aquatic actions. If you’re a Fire Type trainer, you gain resistance to hot temperatures. If you’re a Normal Type trainer, you gain bonuses on basic and normal actions. Etc.
- Field Researcher (10 INT): When you’re collecting samples, running surveys, or doing on-route science, gain advantage on the relevant Intelligence checks.
- Zone Sense (12 INT): You can always attempt an INT check to “read” a route fracture (where the seam is pulling, which exit is real, what’s bait). On success, gain advantage on the next navigation/seam-read check on that route.
- Bond Whisperer (12 INT + Type Specialty): You have a strong read on Pokémon that match your Type Specialty (your “type enthusiast” lane). Gain advantage on attempts to calm, approach, or negotiate with those Pokémon.
- Gadgeteer (12 INT): You can build/repair small field gadgets during rests (detectors, lures, stabilizer tags). The DM sets the materials needed; on a success you gain a small, practical utility item for the next travel/battle scene.
- Iron Will (12 FTD): You can push through one extra training/travel scene when you should be done. You still get the benefit, but you pass out immediately after (you can’t take actions until the next rest/scene transition).
- Battle Analyst (12 INT): Once per battle, you can make an INT check to call the fight. On success, you learn a meaningful tactical truth (best target, likely switch, hidden trick, weakness angle) and an ally gains advantage on one related roll this round.
- Quick Hands (10 ATH): Once per battle, you can use an item (or throw a Poké Ball) without losing your action (DM timing). Doesn’t stack with other “free item” effects.
- Type Specialist (Requires Type Specialty): Your Type Specialty is more than vibes. Gain advantage on checks to identify, track, and work with Pokémon matching your specialty, and gain a 1.5x catch-rate modifier against Pokémon matching your specialty.
- People person (12 SCH): Instantly know when an insight (INT) roll can be used instead of having to initiate it yourself.
- Seam Cartographer (12 INT + Zone Sense): When you travel a route you’ve already mapped, you can reroll one navigation/seam-read check per travel and keep the better result.
- Fracture Runner (12 ATH): When a route becomes a traversal scene (loop risk / wrong-exit / hazard corridor), you can take point and grant the party +1 on the next traversal-related check.
- Stormwise (12 FTD): Gain advantage on checks to endure severe weather. Once per travel, you can reduce the SP cost of one weather hazard by 1 for the party (minimum 0).
- Supply Discipline (10 FTD): Once per travel day, ignore the first “extra supplies” consequence from a failed travel check (you still lose time).
- Field Lab Kit (12 INT + Field Researcher): During a rest you can process samples and bank 1 “Research Note.” Spend a Note to gain advantage on a related knowledge check later (biology, seams, materials).
- Stabilizer Protocols (15 INT): When a High Chaos or fusion event hits, you can attempt an INT check to reduce collateral.
  - On success: reduce one severity (less damage, shorter duration, fewer adds).
- Defuse Assist (12 INT + Gadgeteer): When a fused/rampaging boss is incapacitated and the party attempts a Defuse, you gain advantage on the primary Defuse INT check.
- Raid Captain (12 SCH): Boss battles only. Once per boss battle, you can issue a callout.
  - Effect: one ally gains advantage on their next attack roll/check OR can reposition without provoking (DM timing).
- Tempo Reader (12 INT + Battle Analyst): After the first full round, you can call the enemy’s pattern.
  - Effect: learn one of: its strongest move category, its main weakness angle, or what it will likely do next.
- Emergency Swap (12 ATH): Once per battle, you can switch your active Pokémon without losing your action (still respects any battle system limits).
- Calm Voice (12 SCH): Gain advantage on checks to de-escalate with wild Pokémon and prevent fights.
- Quick Fix (12 INT + Gadgeteer): Once per rest, you can repair one broken item/gear tag without needing a shop.
- Permit Forger (12 INT + Actor/Actress): Gain advantage when using paperwork, disguises, or permits to bypass checkpoints.
- Lockpick & Latch (12 ATH + Quick Hands): Gain advantage on checks to open stuck doors, hatches, lockers, and service panels without making a scene.
- Dual Specialty (15 INT + Expanding Horizons): Choose a second Type Specialty.
  - Your “outsider penalty” applies only to types outside both.
  - If a Pokémon matches either specialty, you get your normal specialty bonus.
  - If a Pokémon matches both (dual-typed), treat it as a double bonus for your specialty effects (double your catch modifier and any check bonuses tied to specialty).
- Terrain Attunement (Requires Type Specialty): When in a biome strongly matching your specialty, once per travel you can reduce the SP cost of one obstacle by 1 (minimum 0).
